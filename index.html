Superheroine

Feminism in DC

CHAPTER ONE: SUPERHERO GENRE

In chapter one titled, the ‘Superhero genre’ Coogan begins with the statement “Wonder Woman is a superheroine, not a female superhero.” [pg 1] proceeding to define the superhero as someone who’s “selflessly using power to improve the community and their outsider status to make things better.” [pg 2] then contrasts the ‘female’ genres of romance, musicals, and melodramas to the more ‘masculine’ genres of adventure, westerns and superhero orientated stories presenting to us the misogynistic stereotyped divide that exists within these genres and the origins of the superhero.

We are presented with historical context into the superhero genre, describing American superheroes as “The sons of Daniel Boone” [pg 2] Daniel Boone was a figure from the 18th-19th century regarded as one of the first folk heroes of America, Coogan describes “The Boone figure” as “the most important hero in American storytelling” [pg 2]. The predecessors to Boone’s narratives in American mythology had previously not had any human heroes, only God was in the position of rescuer, “by replacing God, Boone changed American Mythology, by replacing the classical monomyth structure with the American monomyth” [pg 3].

CHAPTER TWO: THE GENESIS OF WONDERWOMAN

“Wonder Woman is not a daughter of Daniel Boone.” [pg 3], but rather of William Moulton Marston, who’s origins of the character find itself based off of feminist utopian science fiction from the likes of ‘Herland’ and ‘Her in Ourland’ by Charlotte Perkins Gilman.

In this chapter, Coogan properly introduces Wonder Woman as a character within the essay and her standout features against her male counterparts. One of her individualistic differences is that “her diegetic origin story does not follow the hero’s journey.” [pg 4], arguably this makes sense as Coogan soon follows up with “The hero’s journey is a mythological way to teach boys to put their power in service of their community” [pg 4]. Furthermore, because of this difference in Marston’s writing of Wonder Woman, author Carolyn Cocca describes her as ‘antinormative’ [pg 5] finishing with Coogan stating he believes Wonder Woman “Changed the superhero narrative.” [pg 5].

CHAPTER THREE: THE SUPERHEROINE SUBGENRE

“The superheroine subgenre is not about power and responsibility, it is about love.” Culture critic Jennifer K. Stuller exclaims, [pg 5-6]. Throughout this chapter, the differences between the superhero genre and the superheroine genre are outlined, the most notable distinction between the two is the different approaches toward helping their respective communities and the role love plays. Coogan notes how superheroes often are required to leave their community to return with a power or object that will benefit everyone once the hero has returned, meanwhile Wonder Woman’s gifts are from within the community rather than the outside world. She often acts as a mentor to those around her and those she introduces into the community, she teaches others about love and respect for other human beings as well as fulfilling their hierarchy of needs.

“Wonder Woman’s vision is an expression of the ontological vocation of humanisation” [pg 7]

CHAPTER 4: MISUNDERSTANDING MARSTON’S VISION

The central theme of this chapter revolves around how misunderstood Wonder Woman is as a character in modern depictions past Marston’s run, and how these misrepresentations often reject the original feminist messages Marston had. Coogan notes that after Marston’s death, comic writer Robert Kanigher took his position as writer for the series noting he was not a feminist and his transformation of Wonder Woman was “part of the larger postwar domestication movement.” [pg 8]. Since Kanigher’s takeover, Wonder Woman has struggled with being subject to the male gaze, often seen being hypersexualised, unequal to her male companions and in some instances infantilised.

If not just a product of misogyny, her role as a super heroine has also been misconstrued over time. Despite her values of love, mentorship and her role in helping characters up Maslow’s hierarchy of needs; some writers have turned her into a vigilante character. In his work, writer Gregg Rucka has taken Wonder Woman from her values and allowed her to kill, an act Superman and Batman refuse to do. Where these writers often fail in presenting Wonder Woman, comes from the lack of ability to understand her character as a superheroine and not a superhero.

“If she is no longer the ambassador of peace and love, if she is the same as Superman or Batman, then she is no longer the anti-normative superhero Marston created.” [pg 10].

CHAPTER 5: A SENSATIONAL VICTORY

To conclude the essay, a discussion of representation within Wonder Woman is brought into debate. It is promptly noted that overlapping with Brian Azzarello and Cliff Chiang’s run of Wonder Woman in which they move her character away from being a super heroine – editor Kirsty Quinn is placed in charge of a stand-alone comic book series running alongside Azzarello and Chiang’s, Sensation Comics. During Quinn’s run as editor on this series, she brought in mostly female writers who were able to put the super heroine subgenre back into a multitude of stories. Wonder Woman is placed back into her role as a mentor teaching her values to the community, bringing back the original elements of her character.

Coogan once again restates that Wonder Woman’s significance isn’t because she is a female superhero, but because she is a superheroine. The visibility of her gender shouldn’t be the main asset of her character, and that good representation is dependent on how she is written and presented to the viewer. Representation in media matters but picking and choosing characters based on appearance to fill diversity quotas isn’t good enough, “If representational visibility equals power, then almost-naked young white women should be running Western culture.” [Quoted from Peggy Phelan, pg 12]

The essay starts to wrap up by bringing to light the work of Gail Simone, a female writer who wrote for Wonder Woman from 2007 for 30 issues of its run, who was able to demonstrate Marston’s vision by presenting Wonder Woman as a vehicle for change. Simone was able to make a statement with the character, following the previous comic’s arc “Amazon’s attack”, which presented the Amazon women as merciless man killers. She was able to move the story past this disastrous plot point, and years of damaging writing, leaving room for Wonder Woman to grow back into Marston’s vision. “Love, collaboration, companionship and mentorship – Simone reasserts these themes and recuperates Wonder Woman’s story away from the superhero genre and toward the superheroine subgenre.” [pg 13].

“This is the legacy of Wonder Woman. She was not the first heroine, she was not the first female superhero, but she was the first superheroine.” [Pg 13]
