Superheroine

Feminism in DC

  • 1734

    Daniel Boone (1734 - 1820) becomes the father of what we perceive as the modern American superhero, “God was the divine rescuer of the captive. But Boone rescued himself and then his daughter and another girl from captivity. In doing so Boone displaced God as the redeemer. By replacing God, Boone changed American myth.”

    (Coogan, 2018, pg 3)

  • 1941

    Wonder Woman is created by writer William Moulton Marston and artist Harry G. Peter, “Marston drew inspiration from feminist utopian science fiction such as Charlotte Perkins Gilman’s ‘Herland’ (1915) and ‘Her in Ourland’ (1915).

  • 1942

    All-Star Comics 13 (Gardner Fox), “She (wonder woman) is voted the permanent duty of secretary and often stayed behind when the rest of the society rushed off on missions.”

    (Coogan, 2018, pg 7)

  • 1943

    All-Star Comics 17 (Fox, 1943) “Wonder Woman reports to the rest of the JSA that all their meeting notes had been stolen so she returns home to look for them, and the rest of the JSA go off to fight the villainous Brain Wave”

    (Coogan, 2018, pg 7-8)

  • 1947

    William Moulton Marston dies

    Robert Kanigher took over the Wonder Woman comics from Wonder Woman #22, he was not a feminist. “He transformed Wonder Woman at the end of the 1940s as part of the larger postwar domestication movement. She is disempowered, taken away from adventuring, and shifted into romance comics.”

    (Coogan, 2018, pg 8)

  • 1948

    Sensations comics 81 - “Remember, this man’s world of yours will never be without pan and suffering until it learns love, and respect for human rights. Keep your hands extended to all in friendliness but never holding the gun of persecution and intolerance’

    (Moulton and Peters 1948,12)

  • 1994

    William Moulton Marston dies

    Hypersexualised in the 1990’s by William Messn-erLoebs and Mike Deodato - notably in “The contest” arc (Wonder Woman Vol. 2, 1994, 90 -93) “These hypersexualised displays diminish the symbolic threat of their superpowers.”

    (Coogan, 2018, pg 8)

  • 2004

    Michael Turner’s ‘Superman/Batman #10’ 2004, The Amazonian women are “the objects of Superman and Batman’s gaze” (Coogan, 2018, pg 8) As they are seen training “wearing crop tops and mini skirts”

    (Coogan, 2018, pg 8)

  • 2008

    Gail Simone’s run of Wonder Woman (2008 - 2010), “Love, collaboration, companionship and mentorship - Simone reasserts these themes and recuperates Wonder Woman’s story away from the superhero genre and towards the superheroine subgenre”

    (Coogan, 2018, pg 13)

  • 2012

    New 52 David Finch 2012-2016, “Diana’s face is drawn younger and sexier. Her body is spilling out of her top and bottom, and the top and bottom are often emphasized in panels’

    (Lepore, 2017, Superwomen, 50) in (Coogan, 2018, pg 9)

  • 2011

    New 52 Brian Azzarello and Cliff Chiang (2011 - 2014) “With such different and violent foundations for the character, with male gods replacing female Amazons as parents and mentors, her decades-old unique mission to teach Amazonian “lessons of peace and equality” (Superwomen, 49) with an open heart, mind and hand seem to have fallen away. If she is no longer the ambassador of peace and love, if she is the same as Superman or Batman, then she is no longer the anti-normative superhero Marston created; she is no longer critiquing our assumptions about gender but rather conforming to a male norm.”

  • 2015

    Wonder Woman #36 (Williams and Lyle, 2015) “Diana is drawn as though she is constantly on the verge of bursting into tears, with breasts as big as her head, and a waist thinner than her thigh…she looks like a tiny, petulant teenager” (Kremer,2015) in (Coogan, 2018, pg 9) “This portrayal of Wonder Woman demonstrates that many writers and artists do not understand the Marston’s vision for the character nor the superheroine subgenre” (Coogan)
